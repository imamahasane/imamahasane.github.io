## The problem

Lower-dose CT scans are safer for patients — less ionizing radiation — but
the trade-off is noisier raw measurements, which makes reconstructing a
diagnostically useful image harder. Most learned reconstruction methods
treat this as a generic denoising problem: take a noisy image, learn to map
it back to a clean one, using whatever noise model is convenient for
training.

DACD (Dose-Aware Cold Diffusion with Physics Consistency) starts from a
different premise: the noise in a low-dose CT scan isn't generic. It comes
from a specific, well-understood physical process — photon-limited
measurement at the detector — and a reconstruction method that models that
process explicitly should do better than one that doesn't.

## Cold diffusion, briefly

Standard diffusion models (DDPM-style) learn to reverse a fixed corruption
process, typically Gaussian noise added over many steps:

$$
x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\, \epsilon,\qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)
$$

"Cold diffusion" generalizes this idea: the forward corruption process
doesn't have to be Gaussian noise at all — it can be any fixed,
deterministic degradation, as long as a network can learn to invert it step
by step. That flexibility is what makes it a natural fit here: instead of
corrupting a clean CT image with generic noise, DACD's forward process is
built around **Poisson thinning** — the same statistical process that
actually governs photon-limited, low-dose acquisition. The model isn't
learning to reverse an arbitrary corruption; it's learning to reverse the
specific kind of information loss a real low-dose scan undergoes.

## Physics consistency

On top of that, DACD adds an explicit consistency constraint tying each
reconstruction step back to the measured sinogram — the raw scanner data —
rather than letting the model freely hallucinate plausible-looking anatomy.
This is the part that matters most for a clinical setting: a reconstruction
that looks sharp but drifts from what the scanner actually measured is a
liability, not an improvement.

## Why this combination helps

Matching the forward process to the true physical degradation, plus
constraining the reverse process to stay consistent with the actual
measurement, is what produces both results reported for DACD: a PSNR
improvement over prior state-of-the-art methods, and meaningfully faster
inference. The speedup follows fairly directly from cold diffusion's
structure — a well-matched forward process needs fewer reverse steps to
invert than a generic one does.
